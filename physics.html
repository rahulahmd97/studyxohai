To determine the condition under which the rate of stimulated emission equals the rate of spontaneous emission, we use the Einstein coefficients and Planck's law of radiation.

1. Rate Equations
The rates for these two processes are defined as:
- Spontaneous Emission Rate: $R_{\text{spont}} = N_2 A_{21}$
- Stimulated Emission Rate: $R_{\text{stim}} = N_2 B_{21} \rho(\nu)$
Where:
- $N_2$ is the number of atoms in the excited state.
- $A_{21}$ and $B_{21}$ are Einstein coefficients.
- $\rho(\nu)$ is the energy density of the radiation field at frequency $\nu$.

2. The Equality Condition
Setting the two rates equal:
$$ N_2 B_{21} \rho(\nu) = N_2 A_{21} $$
$$ \rho(\nu) = \frac{A_{21}}{B_{21}} $$

3. Relation between Einstein Coefficients
From Einstein's theory, the ratio of the coefficients is:
$$ \frac{A_{21}}{B_{21}} = \frac{8\pi h \nu^3}{c^3} $$
Also, according to Planck's Law, the energy density is:
$$ \rho(\nu) = \frac{8\pi h \nu^3}{c^3} \frac{1}{e^{h\nu/kT} - 1} $$

4. Final Derivation
Substituting the ratio back into the equality condition:
$$ \frac{8\pi h \nu^3}{c^3} \frac{1}{e^{h\nu/kT} - 1} = \frac{8\pi h \nu^3}{c^3} $$
By canceling common terms on both sides, we get:
$$ \frac{1}{e^{h\nu/kT} - 1} = 1 $$
$$ e^{h\nu/kT} - 1 = 1 $$
$$ e^{h\nu/kT} = 2 $$
Taking the natural logarithm on both sides:
$$ \frac{h\nu}{kT} = \ln(2) $$

Conclusion: The condition for stimulated emission to equal spontaneous emission is:
$$ h\nu = kT \ln(2) $$
Or approximately: $h\nu \approx 0.693kT$

Solution:
For a particle in a 1D infinite potential well, the energy of the $n^{th}$ level is: $$ E_n = n^2 E_1 $$ where $E_1$ is the ground state energy ($n=1$).

Q3.
(a) An electron has speed of $6.6 \times 10^4$ m/s with an accuracy of 0.01%. Calculate the uncertainty in position of an electron. (Given $m_e = 9.1 \times 10^{-31}$ kg and Planck's constant $h = 6.6 \times 10^{-34}$ Js). mark 4 ASTU 2023

See Answer

Solution:
Using Heisenberg's Uncertainty Principle: $$ \Delta x \cdot \Delta p \geq \frac{h}{4\pi} $$ For numerical problems, we often use the simplified form $\Delta x \cdot \Delta p \approx h$.

Given data:
Speed of electron, $v = 6.6 \times 10^4 \text{ m/s}$
Accuracy = 0.01%
Mass of electron, $m_e = 9.1 \times 10^{-31} \text{ kg}$
Planck's constant, $h = 6.6 \times 10^{-34} \text{ Js}$

Step 1: Find uncertainty in velocity
$$ \Delta v = 0.01\% \text{ of } v = \frac{0.01}{100} \times 6.6 \times 10^4 $$ $$ \Delta v = 10^{-4} \times 6.6 \times 10^4 = 6.6 \text{ m/s} $$

Step 2: Find uncertainty in momentum
$$ \Delta p = m_e \cdot \Delta v = 9.1 \times 10^{-31} \times 6.6 $$ $$ \Delta p = 6.006 \times 10^{-30} \text{ kg m/s} $$

Step 3: Find uncertainty in position
$$ \Delta x \geq \frac{h}{\Delta p} = \frac{6.6 \times 10^{-34}}{6.006 \times 10^{-30}} $$ $$ \Delta x \geq 1.099 \times 10^{-4} \text{ m} $$

Final Answer: $$ \Delta x \approx 1.1 \times 10^{-4} \text{ m} $$ Note: If using $\Delta x \cdot \Delta p \geq \frac{h}{4\pi}$, then $\Delta x \geq 8.8 \times 10^{-6} \text{ m}$

Q5.
(a) What is phase velocity? Show that the group velocity associated with a wave packet is equal to the velocity of the particle. mark 5 ASTU 2021

See Answer

Phase Velocity and Group Velocity

(a) Phase Velocity
Phase velocity ($v_p$) is the rate at which the phase of a single frequency wave propagates through space. It is the velocity of any one constant phase point of the wave (e.g., the crest).

Mathematically: $$ v_p = \frac{\omega}{k} $$ where $\omega$ is the angular frequency and $k$ is the wave number.

Showing $v_g = v_{particle}$
To show that the group velocity ($v_g$) of a wave packet is equal to the particle velocity ($v$), we use the de Broglie relations:

  1. Energy: $E = \hbar\omega \implies \omega = \frac{E}{\hbar}$
  2. Momentum: $p = \hbar k \implies k = \frac{p}{\hbar}$

The group velocity is defined as the derivative of angular frequency with respect to the wave number: $$ v_g = \frac{d\omega}{dk} $$

Substituting the de Broglie relations: $$ v_g = \frac{d(E/\hbar)}{d(p/\hbar)} = \frac{dE}{dp} $$

For a non-relativistic particle of mass $m$, the kinetic energy is: $$ E = \frac{p^2}{2m} $$

Differentiating energy with respect to momentum: $$ \frac{dE}{dp} = \frac{d}{dp} \left( \frac{p^2}{2m} \right) = \frac{2p}{2m} = \frac{p}{m} $$

Since momentum $p = mv$, we have: $$ v_g = \frac{mv}{m} = v $$

Conclusion: Thus, the group velocity of the wave packet is exactly equal to the velocity of the particle.

Q7.
(a) Discuss the properties of a wave packet representing a quantum particle. mark 5 ASTU 2019

See Answer

A wave packet is a localized disturbance formed by the superposition of many individual waves of slightly different wavelengths. In quantum mechanics, it represents a particle, like an electron, balancing the wave-particle duality.

Here are the key properties:

1. Localization
Unlike a pure sine wave (which extends to infinity), a wave packet is localized in space. This means the probability of finding the particle (governed by $|\psi|^2$) is high only within a finite region of space, matching our classical intuition of a "particle."

2. Superposition Principle
A wave packet is mathematically constructed by summing (integrating) multiple plane waves.

  • Formula: $\psi(x,t) = \int A(k)e^{i(kx-\omega t)} dk$
  • The range of wave numbers ($\Delta k$) determines how tightly the packet is "packed."

3. Dual Velocities
Wave packets are characterized by two distinct velocities:

  • Phase Velocity ($v_p$): The speed of an individual wave component within the packet ($v_p = \omega/k$).
  • Group Velocity ($v_g$): The speed at which the overall envelope (and thus the particle/energy) moves ($v_g = d\omega/dk$). For a quantum particle, the group velocity equals the classical particle velocity ($v_g = v_{particle}$).

4. Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle
The wave packet directly illustrates the uncertainty principle.

  • To make a packet more localized in space (small $\Delta x$), you must include a wider range of wavelengths (large $\Delta k$, and thus large $\Delta p$).
  • Relationship: $\Delta x \cdot \Delta p \geq \frac{\hbar}{2}$

5. Dispersion
In most media (and for free particles in vacuum), different wavelengths travel at different speeds. As a result, the wave packet spreads out over time. This means the certainty of the particle's position decreases as it moves.
