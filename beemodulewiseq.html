An ideal voltage source theoretically provides a constant voltage regardless of the current drawn, possessing zero internal resistance. In contrast, a practical voltage source has a non-zero internal resistance in series, causing its terminal voltage to drop as the current drawn by a load increases.

The following breakdown outlines the key differences between these two concepts:

(b) State and explain superposition theorem.    mark 3
See Answer

The superposition theorem states that in any linear electrical circuit with multiple sources, the total current or voltage across any component is the algebraic sum of the individual contributions from each source acting alone.

How It Works (Step-by-Step)

To find the voltage or current of a specific component using this theorem:

  1. Isolate One Source: Choose one independent voltage or current source to analyze.
  2. Deactivate Other Sources: Turn off all other independent sources in the circuit:
    • Replace voltage sources with a short circuit (0 V).
    • Replace current sources with an open circuit (0 A).
    • Note: Dependent sources (sources that rely on other circuit values) are never turned off.
  3. Calculate the Response: Solve for the current or voltage across your target component caused by the single active source.
  4. Repeat: Do this for every independent source in the circuit.
  5. Add Algebraically: Combine all the individual voltage or current values to get the final total. Be sure to pay attention to positive and negative signs based on direction/polarity.

2. Explain with the help of the power diagram that the value of average power drawn by the pure inductive circuit during one cycle is zero.        mark 4 ASTU 2025

See Answer

In a pure inductive circuit, the current ($I$) lags behind the voltage ($V$) by a phase angle of 90° ($\pi/2$).

1. Mathematical Background

If the instantaneous voltage is $v = V_m\sin(\omega t)$, then the instantaneous current is:

$$ i = I_m\sin\left(\omega t - \frac{\pi}{2}\right) = -I_m\cos(\omega t) $$

The instantaneous power $p$ is the product of $v$ and $i$:

$$ p = vi = (V_m\sin\omega t)(-I_m\cos\omega t) $$

Using the identity $2\sin\theta\cos\theta = \sin 2\theta$, we get:

$$ p = -\frac{V_m I_m}{2}\sin(2\omega t) $$

2. Explanation via Power Diagram

The power diagram (waveform) reveals why the average power is zero over a full cycle:

  • Frequency: The power wave oscillates at twice the frequency of the voltage and current.
  • Symmetry: For every half-cycle of the power wave, the circuit absorbs energy from the source (positive power) to build the magnetic field in the inductor. In the following half-cycle, the magnetic field collapses, and the energy is returned to the source (negative power).
  • Net Result: The area under the positive loops of the power curve exactly equals the area under the negative loops.

3. Conclusion

Mathematically, the average power ($P_{avg}$) over one full period ($T$) is:

$$ P_{avg} = \frac{1}{T}\int_{0}^{T} -\frac{V_m I_m}{2}\sin(2\omega t)\,dt = 0 $$

Graphically, since the positive and negative halves of the power cycle are identical, the net energy consumed over one complete cycle is zero.
